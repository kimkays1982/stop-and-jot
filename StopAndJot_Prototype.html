"Tornado"
A tornado is a type of violent windstorm in which a rapidly rotating column of air extends from the base of a thunderstorm to the ground. Like a hurricane, a tornado is a type of cyclone. A cyclone is a low-pressure area with winds spiraling around the center. A low-pressure area is a region in which warm, light air is rising into the atmosphere.
Tornadoes can be various sizes, shapes, and colors. They can occur in most parts of the world, except in the polar regions. Most tornadoes, however, occur in the United States, where they are sometimes called twisters. Most tornadoes in the United States occur between March and June. But they can happen at any time of year.
Tornadoes typically develop within a large low-pressure system. The winds circling around this "low" bring warm, moist air northward from the Gulf of Mexico. They also bring cool, dry air from the west. The warm air flows in from the south. The cold, dry air flows in from the west. This creates the conditions for a large storm system from the south and west.
As these streams of air converge, conditions are just right for powerful thunderstorms that can develop. Under certain conditions, smaller rotations, called vortices, develop inside the larger circulation.
These types of conditions often occur in an area that runs northeastward across the Great Plains of the United States are Kansas, Missouri, Nebraska, and Iowa.
Most tornadoes begin high up in a large thundercloud. At times, the rotating air begins to descend from the base of the thundercloud. As it descends down from the thundercloud, it forms a funnel that reaches the ground. The only clue that a tornado is approaching from a distance is a dark, funnel-shaped cloud.
